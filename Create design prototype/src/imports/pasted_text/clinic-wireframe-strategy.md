Landing Page Architecture: Local Medical Clinic — Jordan Market
Section-by-Section Wireframe Strategy

Section 1 — Sticky Navigation Bar
Goal: Establish instant brand trust and ensure frictionless access to critical actions at all times, regardless of scroll depth.
Key Elements:

Clinic logo (left-aligned)
Navigation links: Home / Services / Doctors / Insurance / Contact
Phone number with click-to-call icon (prominent, right side)
WhatsApp icon button (separate, green-colored)
Primary CTA button: "Book an Appointment"

Local Context: Jordanian patients rely heavily on direct phone calls and WhatsApp for medical inquiries before committing. Placing both contact channels in the nav bar eliminates friction immediately. A sticky bar ensures the booking CTA is never out of reach while scrolling.

Section 2 — Hero Section
Goal: Capture attention within 3 seconds, communicate who you are, and direct the visitor toward the single most important action — booking.
Key Elements:

Bold headline: Communicates the core promise (e.g., "Your Health, Cared for in Amman")
Subheadline: Specialty focus + reassurance (e.g., "Specialized care across [X] medical departments, staffed by board-certified physicians")
Two CTAs side by side: "Book an Appointment" (primary) + "WhatsApp Us Now" (secondary)
Hero image: Warm, professional photo of a doctor-patient interaction (not a stock cold-clinic photo)
Trust badge strip below CTAs: "10+ Years of Practice · 3 Clinic Locations · Insurance Accepted · Open 7 Days"
Optional: Subtle Arabic typography accent to reinforce local identity

Local Context: In Jordan, personal trust in a healthcare provider is paramount. A human, warm image of an actual doctor (or a close approximation) builds familiarity faster than clinical imagery. The dual CTA respects that many patients prefer WhatsApp over formal booking flows.

Section 3 — Social Proof Bar (Above the Fold Anchor)
Goal: Immediately neutralize skepticism with hard, verifiable numbers before the visitor scrolls further.
Key Elements:

4 metric tiles displayed horizontally:

+8,000 Patients Treated
+15 Specialist Doctors
3 Clinic Branches (Amman / Zarqa / Irbid)
98% Patient Satisfaction Rate


Short line beneath: "Trusted by families across Jordan since [Year]"

Local Context: Jordanians respond strongly to community-level trust signals. Mentioning multiple city branches tells a patient in Zarqa or Irbid that this clinic serves them, not just Amman's west side.

Section 4 — Services Section
Goal: Quickly show the full scope of available care so no visitor self-disqualifies by assuming the clinic doesn't offer their needed service.
Key Elements:

Section headline: "What We Treat" or "Our Medical Specialties"
6–9 Service Cards in a grid layout, each containing:

Department icon (medical-themed)
Department name (e.g., Internal Medicine, Pediatrics, Dermatology, Orthopedics, OB-GYN, Dental)
One-line description
Micro-CTA: "Learn More" or "Book for This Department"


"View All Services →" link below the grid

Local Context: Jordanian patients often visit a general clinic for multi-family needs — parents, children, and elderly relatives. Showing pediatrics, OB-GYN, and internal medicine together signals a family-oriented practice, which is a very strong motivator in the local market.

Section 5 — Meet the Doctors
Goal: Humanize the clinic. In healthcare, people choose doctors, not brands. This section converts trust into intent.
Key Elements:

Section headline: "Our Medical Team"
3–4 Doctor Profile Cards (the most senior/prominent doctors):

Professional headshot (essential)
Doctor name + title (Dr. [Name], MD)
Specialty + years of experience
Education credentials (e.g., "Graduated from University of Jordan, Fellowship in Germany")
Languages spoken (Arabic / English — very relevant for patients)
CTA: "Book with Dr. [Name]"


"See Full Team →" link

Local Context: In Jordan, academic credentials from known institutions (University of Jordan, Jordan University of Science and Technology, or international fellowship locations like Germany or the UK) carry enormous weight. Listing them builds instant credibility. Language capability matters too, as some elderly patients are more comfortable in Arabic only.

Section 6 — Why Choose Us (Differentiators)
Goal: Address the "why you over the other clinic down the street?" objection proactively.
Key Elements:

Section headline: "Why Families in Jordan Trust Us"
3-column icon + text layout:

🕐 Extended Hours — Open 7 days, early morning to late evening
🏥 Modern Equipment — Digital diagnostics, in-house lab & imaging
💳 Insurance Coverage — Accepted: Royal Medical Services, UNRWA, Mednet, plus major private insurers
📍 Accessible Locations — Branches in Amman, Zarqa, and Irbid
🌐 Bilingual Staff — Arabic & English speaking doctors and reception
⏱️ Short Wait Times — Online booking reduces lobby waiting



Local Context: Insurance compatibility is a critical decision factor in Jordan. Explicitly naming Royal Medical Services (military/government employees) and UNRWA (refugee community) alongside private insurers demonstrates inclusivity and removes a major conversion barrier. Extended hours also matter for working families.

Section 7 — Patient Testimonials
Goal: Use social proof from real patients to eliminate residual doubt and reinforce emotional trust.
Key Elements:

Section headline: "What Our Patients Say"
3 testimonial cards (carousel or static grid):

Patient first name + neighborhood/city (e.g., "Rania M., Abdoun, Amman")
Star rating (5-star visual)
Testimonial text (2–4 lines, focused on a specific experience)
Department visited (e.g., "Pediatrics")


Google Reviews aggregate rating badge (e.g., ⭐ 4.8 / 5 — 320 Google Reviews)
Link: "Read More Reviews on Google"

Local Context: Neighborhood specificity (Abdoun, Sweifieh, Marka, Zarqa) makes testimonials feel authentic and geographically relatable. Jordanian patients heavily rely on word-of-mouth and community recommendations — testimonials digitize that behavior. The Google Reviews badge provides a verifiable third-party anchor.

Section 8 — Insurance & Payment Section
Goal: Remove one of the single biggest conversion blockers in the Jordanian healthcare market — payment uncertainty.
Key Elements:

Section headline: "We Work With Your Insurance"
Logo strip of accepted insurance providers:

Royal Medical Services (RMS)
UNRWA
Jordan Islamic Insurance
Arab Orient Insurance
Mednet / AXA / other common providers


Short copy: "Not sure if your plan is covered? Our reception team will confirm within minutes."
Secondary CTA: "Ask About Your Coverage" → links to WhatsApp or the contact form

Local Context: A very large portion of Jordan's population (military families, government employees, UNRWA beneficiaries) depends on specific insurance schemes. Making this visible reduces inbound "do you accept my insurance?" calls and increases booking confidence significantly.

Section 9 — ✉️ Send Us a Message (Lead Capture / Email Inquiry Form)
Goal: Capture leads from visitors who are interested but not yet ready to call or book — turn passive interest into a trackable, actionable inquiry.
Key Elements:

Section headline: "Have a Question? Send Us a Message"
Subheadline: "Our team will respond within a few hours — in Arabic or English"
Form fields:

Full Name (required)
Phone Number (required) — pre-filled format +962 for Jordan
Email Address (required)
Preferred Clinic Branch — dropdown: Amman / Zarqa / Irbid / Any
Department / Specialty of Interest — dropdown list
Message / Question (textarea)
Preferred Contact Method — radio buttons: Phone Call / WhatsApp / Email


Submit CTA button: "Send My Inquiry"
Privacy micro-copy below button: "Your information is private and will never be shared."
Alongside the form — a small info panel with:

📞 Main phone number (click-to-call)
💬 WhatsApp number (click-to-chat)
📧 Clinic email address
⏰ Response time promise: "We reply within 2–4 hours during working hours"



Local Context: Many Jordanian patients are hesitant to call a clinic without knowing if they'll reach someone immediately. A written inquiry form removes phone anxiety. The "Preferred Contact Method" field is a high-trust UX move — it tells the patient: "we will reach you the way YOU want." Including the +962 prefix pre-filled removes friction for mobile users. The Arabic/English bilingual note signals that the response will be in the patient's comfort language.

Section 10 — Location & Map Section
Goal: Eliminate the "where exactly are you?" barrier — a major drop-off point for local service businesses.
Key Elements:

Section headline: "Find Your Nearest Branch"
Tab or accordion switcher: Amman Branch / Zarqa Branch / Irbid Branch
For each branch:

Embedded Google Map (interactive)
Full street address in Arabic and English
Nearest landmark (e.g., "Opposite ABC Mall, 2nd Circle")
Working hours (daily schedule)
Direct branch phone number
"Get Directions" button → opens Google Maps app


WhatsApp button per branch

Local Context: In Jordan, landmarks are more navigable than street addresses for many patients. Saying "near Mecca Street" or "opposite the 4th Circle Mosque" is genuinely more useful than a street number. Google Maps integration is essential — it's the dominant navigation tool across all demographics in Jordan.

Section 11 — Final CTA Banner (Pre-Footer)
Goal: Re-engage scrollers who consumed all content but haven't converted yet — one last, clear moment of decision.
Key Elements:

Bold headline: "Ready to Book Your Appointment?"
Subtext: "Join thousands of families who trust us with their health"
Two CTAs side by side: "Book Online Now" + "Call Us Directly"
Background: A strong brand color (not white) to visually interrupt the scroll and signal a new decision moment

Local Context: Many Jordanian users scroll completely before acting. This banner serves as a final, low-pressure re-engagement moment — the dual CTA again respects both digital and phone-first personas.

Section 12 — Footer
Goal: Provide navigational utility, legal coverage, and final trust signals.
Key Elements:

Logo + short clinic tagline
Quick links: Home / Services / Doctors / Insurance / Privacy Policy / Terms
Branch addresses (condensed, all three)
Social media icons: Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp (the primary channels in Jordan)
Copyright line
Optional: Ministry of Health accreditation badge or Jordanian Medical Association number

Local Context: Facebook and Instagram remain the dominant social platforms in Jordan. A Ministry of Health or JMA credential in the footer acts as a regulatory trust stamp — especially reassuring to first-time patients or those coming from referrals.
